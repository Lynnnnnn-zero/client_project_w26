Portfolios

What a Portfolio Is (and What It Is Not)

A portfolio is a curated set of examples that helps employers understand your skills through real work. It is not only a gallery of final visuals. Strong portfolios show your process, decision-making, and impact.

For UMSI students, a portfolio can include course projects, personal projects, research work, client work, and other experiences that demonstrate relevant skills.

Start with a Simple Structure

  1. Choose your goal: Decide what roles you are targeting and what skills you want to show.
  2. Select 3-5 projects: Pick work that best matches your goal (quality over quantity).
  3. Write one short case study per project: Explain the problem, your role, your process, and the outcome.
  4. Add supporting artifacts: Screenshots, diagrams, links, or short demos that strengthen your story.
  5. Make it easy to contact you: Include a clear way to reach you and link to your resume if helpful.

What to Include in Each Case Study

  • Context: What problem were you solving, and for whom?
  • Your role: What did you do specifically (especially in team projects)?
  • Process: What steps did you take (research, ideation, prototyping, testing, iteration)?
  • Key decisions: What tradeoffs did you make, and why?
  • Outcome: What changed? What did you deliver? What impact did your work have?
  • Reflection: What would you do differently next time?

Portfolio Content Ideas by Career Direction

Your portfolio does not need to look the same as someone else's. Choose artifacts that match the type of role you want.

  • UX / Product Design: Case studies with research insights, user needs, iterations, and final flows.
  • Data / Analytics: Clear problem framing, methods, visualizations, and what decisions the analysis supported.
  • Information Architecture / Content Strategy: Before/after structure, taxonomy decisions, and usability considerations.
  • Research: Research questions, study design, findings, and how insights informed recommendations.

Common Mistakes to Avoid

  • Only showing final screens: Employers also want to see how you think and how you got there.
  • Unclear ownership: If it was a group project, clearly state what you personally contributed.
  • Too many projects with little depth: A few strong case studies are better than many shallow ones.
  • Missing outcomes: Include results whenever possible (metrics, feedback, what improved, what shipped).
  • Hard-to-scan pages: Use headings and short sections so readers can scan quickly.
